## Engineering Philosophy

**On Software Engineering**
Software engineering is fundamentally about managing complexity and enabling sustainable change. Great engineers write code that is not just correct, but maintainable, testable, and clearly communicates intent to future readers. Architecture matters more than any individual line of code because it enables or constrains future change.

**On Technical Leadership**
Technical leaders serve engineers, not command them. Our role is to establish clarity, remove obstacles, advocate for engineering perspectives, and enable engineers to do their best work. Technical depth remains essential—credibility is earned through understanding the problems teams face.

**On Code Quality**
Code quality is not optional; it's a business requirement. Technical debt compounds exponentially. Investing in testing, refactoring, and clear architecture early prevents expensive rework later. TDD and comprehensive testing practices are not luxuries but investments in long-term velocity.

**On Architecture**
Architecture is about managing dependencies and enabling change. Good architecture enables multiple teams to work independently, supports multiple deployment models, and facilitates testing. Modular design, clear interfaces, and separation of concerns enable sustainable growth.

**On Mentoring**
People development is the highest-leverage activity in leadership. Time invested in mentoring multiplies through the impact mentees have on their teams and organizations. Mentoring includes technical guidance, leadership coaching, and career development.

**On AI & Automation**
AI and automation are force multipliers, not job eliminators. They free engineers from boilerplate work enabling focus on problems where human creativity and judgment matter. However, AI has limitations; humans remain essential for architectural decisions, edge case handling, and complex problem-solving.

**On Delivery**
Delivering is what matters; plans rarely survive contact with reality. We adapt based on learning, communicate changes clearly, and maintain momentum. Predictability comes from understanding capacity, being honest about complexity, and maintaining sustainable pace.
